COPD Patient Clustering

Author: Kyler Fung
Project Type: Machine Learning • Unsupervised Learning • Healthcare Analytics

Project Summary

Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) remains a leading cause of morbidity and motrality worldwide. Advances in medical treatment and disease management have improved patient outcomes and extended life expectancy, raising the question of whether previosuly identified COPD patient subgroups remain representative of the current population. In this study, we applied an unsupervised machine learning framework combining Random Forest-based prosimity measures with the PArtitioning Around Medoids (PAM) clustering algorithm to identify distinct patient phenotypes. Our analysis identified four stable patient clusters, consistent with findings from prebious research, suggesting that the underlying clinical subgroup structure has remained largely unchanged despite advances in treatment. Future work will focus on characterizing these clusters through demographic and clinical variables to better understand the features that distinguish each patient group.

Overview

Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) is a progessive respiratory disease that restricts airflow and makes breathing increasingly difficult. It is one of the leading causes of death worldwide, accounting for approximately 3.7 million deaths each year, including more than 140,000 deaths annually in the United States. COPD primarily consists of two conditions: emphysema, which destroys the alveoli (air sacs) responsible for gas exchange, and chronic bronchitis, which causes persistent inflammation of the bronchial tubes. Most patients exhibit characteristics of both diseases

Although treatments can improve quality of life and slow disease progression, there is currently no cure for COPD. Because patients epxerience the diease differently, identifying clinically meaningful patient subgroups can help physicians tailor treatments and improve long-term outcomes.

Previous studies have identified four major COPD Patient phenotypes based on pulmonary function and clinical characteristics. However, advances in medical care and disease management may have altered these patient populations over time. This project investigates whether the underlying subgroup structure remains consistent in a modern COPD cohort.

Dataset

The study analyzed 5,797 COPD patients. Due to privacy and an unbiased perspective, we gnereated a large collection of derived pulmonary functuion features from spirometry measurements to reduce bias introduced by manually selected variables

The dataset includes measurements describing:

  • Lung capacity (VC, FEV1, FEV3, FEV6)
  • Peak expiratory flow
  • Flow-volume curve characteristics
  • Airway obstruction scores
  • Statistical desscriptors (mean, variance, skewness, kurtosis)
  • Spline-based curve freatures
  • Temporal Breathing characteristics
  • Additional engineered respiratory biomarkers

In total, the analysis incorporated over 150 engineered features extracted from each patient's spirometry curve.
